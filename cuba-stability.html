🏛️ Regime Stability & Leadership
▼

Cuba is governed by the Communist Party of Cuba under President Miguel Díaz-Canel, who succeeded Raúl Castro in 2021. The Castro era formally ended but the party apparatus, military, and MININT (Ministry of Interior) retain control. The regime faces its most serious legitimacy crisis in decades following the 2021 protests.

11J Protests (2021): The July 11, 2021 protests were the largest in Cuba since 1994, erupting across dozens of cities in response to economic hardship and blackouts. The government responded with mass arrests and sentences of up to 25 years for protesters. Over 1,000 remain imprisoned.

💡 Energy Crisis & Economic Collapse
▼

Cuba faces a severe energy crisis with rolling blackouts lasting 12–20 hours per day in many provinces. The electricity grid relies on aging Soviet-era infrastructure and deteriorating fuel imports from Venezuela and Russia. In 2024, the national grid collapsed multiple times leaving the entire island without power for days.

Economic conditions include hyperinflation, severe shortages of food and medicine, and a rapidly shrinking economy. The U.S. embargo and loss of Venezuelan subsidized oil have compounded the crisis. Remittances from the Cuban diaspora are a lifeline.

🇺🇸 U.S.–Cuba Relations
▼

U.S.–Cuba relations remain deeply adversarial. The U.S. embargo (in place since 1962) continues. Cuba is designated a State Sponsor of Terrorism by the U.S. The Trump administration reinstated and expanded Cuba on the SST list. The U.S. Embassy in Havana operates with a reduced staff and has limited consular services.

Migration: Cuba's economic and political crisis has driven record migration to the United States, with hundreds of thousands arriving via the southern border or through the CBP One parole program. The U.S.–Cuba relationship is heavily shaped by the Cuban-American diaspora's political influence in Florida.

🤝 Humanitarian Conditions
▼

Cuba's healthcare system — once a point of national pride — has deteriorated sharply due to lack of medicines, equipment, and trained personnel who have emigrated. Food insecurity is widespread; Cubans spend the majority of income on basic food items. The government rations system (libreta) no longer adequately covers nutritional needs.

Migration wave: Cuba lost an estimated 500,000+ people between 2022 and 2024 — roughly 5% of its population — to emigration. This represents a historic demographic drain of working-age Cubans.
